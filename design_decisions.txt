Question 1: Fact Table Grain
Decision 1: Fact Table Grain
Choice: Option A: One row per encounter
Why: The primary business process being measured is the patient encounter. Queries 1, 3, and 4 all aggregate data at
the encounter level (monthly encounters, inpatient readmissions, and total billing per encounter). Choosing Option A
establishes a clear, consistent grain for the fact_encounters table. If we chose Option B or C, we would artificially
inflate the row count (row explosion) and make calculating encounter-level totals (like revenue or unique patients)
much more difficult and prone to double-counting errors

Decision 3: Pre-Aggregated Metrics
Storing pre-calculated metrics directly in the fact table shifts the heavy lifting from read-time to write-time. That
is, during read-time, queries can simply pull these pre-aggregated values without needing to perform expensive
calculations or joins. It also ensures computations are done in a single place during ETL, which promotes consistency
and reduces the risk of errors in calculations across different queries.

- diagnosis_count & procedure_count: Useful for quick clinical complexity reporting without having to count rows in
junction tables.

- total_claim_amount & total_allowed_amount: By pulling these in from the billing table during ETL, we eliminate the
need to join a separate billing table entirely (Solves Q4).

- readmission_30_day_flag: Pre-calculating whether an encounter resulted in a 30-day readmission
during the ETL pipeline completely eliminates the massive self-join bottleneck identified in Q3.

- length_of_stay_days: Pre-calculating the date difference between admission and discharge avoids runtime date math.



Decision 4: Bridge Tables
CHOICE: Yes, we will use Bridge Tables (bridge_encounter_diagnosis and bridge_encounter_procedure).
WHY: Healthcare encounters inherently have a many-to-many relationship with diagnoses and procedures.
If we put these directly into the main fact table, we would violate our grain (Option A) and cause a
Cartesian product (row explosion) just like we saw in Q2. By using Bridge Tables, the fact table safely remains
at one row per encounter. When we need to analyze diagnoses or procedures, we can join through the bridge tables using
fast, indexed surrogate keys, keeping the dimensional model clean and preventing double-counting of revenue or patient
metrics.
